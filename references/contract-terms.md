## Pricing Formulas

### Original Schedule (Jan 2019 - Dec 2023)
Exact language: "Materials are billed at cost plus wastage for generic stock. Specifically, the wastage charge is 10% for any generic paper stock and 5% for generic envelope stock."
- No margin component
- Wastage: 10% paper, 5% envelopes
- Effective rate for envelopes: 5.0% over vendor

### Amendment No. 1 (Jan 2024 - present)
Exact language: "Materials are billed at inventory cost plus 10% margin. Inventory cost means for (i) Client specific inventory: vendor price; and (ii) generic inventory: vendor price plus wastage as follows: 10% for continuous form, 3% for cutsheet, and 2% for envelopes."
- Wastage (generic only): 10% continuous form, 3% cutsheet, 2% envelopes
- Margin: 10% on inventory cost
- Client-specific inventory cost = vendor price (no wastage added)
- Generic inventory cost = vendor price + wastage
- Effective rate for generic envelopes: (vendor + 2%) x 1.10 = 12.2% over vendor

### Client-Specific vs Generic Classification
Both the original schedule and Amendment No. 1 contain identical billing basis language:
- "For generic stock, the unit rate will be billed based on **usage**."
- "For Client specific stock, the unit rate will be based on **receipt** of such stock."

This distinction has been in the contract since January 2019. The classification determines both the billing basis (usage vs receipt) and whether wastage is included in inventory cost.

### Actual Markup (Broadridge Practice)

For envelopes (classified by Broadridge as client-specific):

| Component | Contract (if generic) | Broadridge Actual (client-specific) | Delta |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|-------|
| Wastage | +2% on vendor price | Not applied (correct for client-specific) | (2%) |
| Margin | +10% on inventory cost | +10% on vendor price | -- |
| **Effective rate** | **12.2%** | **10.0%** | **-2.2%** |

Note: If envelopes are correctly classified as client-specific, the 10% markup with no wastage is contractually correct. The key dispute is whether unbranded standard envelopes should be classified as client-specific or generic.

## Billing Basis

Both original contract (Section 4) and Amendment No. 1 state:
- Generic stock: "the unit rate will be billed based on **usage**"
- Client-specific stock: "the unit rate will be based on **receipt** of such stock"

**Actual practice:** D17 invoice "ICS ENVELOPE CHARGES" line items match purchase report invoiced totals exactly (receipt-based billing).
- Correlation: invoiced vs purchased r=0.94, invoiced vs used r=0.65
- 2 months with zero purchases but 250K+ usage showed $0 billed

**Key question:** Broadridge classifies our envelopes as client-specific (receipt-based billing is correct under that classification). Our position is that unbranded, standard-size envelopes should be generic stock (usage-based). If reclassified:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Actual invoiced (receipt-based) | $1,575,143 |
| If billed on usage (generic terms) | $1,382,771 |
| Difference | **$192,372** |

Note: This $192K figure is only a discrepancy if the envelopes should be classified as generic. Under client-specific classification, receipt-based billing is contractually correct.

## Wastage Discrepancy

Under the amendment, contractual wastage for envelopes (if generic) is 2%. Koebel stated operational wastage is 10-15%.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Contract wastage for envelopes (generic, amendment) | 2% |
| Koebel-stated operational wastage | 10-15% |
| Actual wastage at 10% (Broadridge low) | 1,846,995 envelopes |
| Actual wastage at 15% (Broadridge high) | 2,770,492 envelopes |
| Excess beyond 2% contract max (~369K) at 10% | ~1.5M envelopes |
| Excess beyond 2% contract max (~369K) at 15% | ~2.4M envelopes |

Note: Under client-specific classification, wastage is not separately charged (it's absorbed by Broadridge or passed through in purchase volumes). The wastage discrepancy is primarily relevant if envelopes are reclassified to generic, where the 2% surcharge is meant to cover wastage and anything above 2% is Broadridge's cost to bear.

## Audit Rights

- MSA Section 23.S: Apex can inspect books/records to verify "Service volumes and fees," including "fees charged to Client"
- MSA Section 23.S: Broadridge must provide Apex's auditors "any reasonable additional information and assistance"
- Amendment Section 4: Broadridge must provide "monthly invoices detailing fees and the passthrough-costs"
- MSA Section 4.D: invoices payable "subject to a bona fide dispute"
- Schedule Section 6(g): non-waiver clause — Apex hasn't lost rights by not previously auditing
